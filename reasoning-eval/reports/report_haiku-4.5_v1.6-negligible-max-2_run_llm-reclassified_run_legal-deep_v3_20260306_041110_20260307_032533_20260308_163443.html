Third-Party Consent: Georgia v. Randolph ### The Cohabitant Consent Problem **Georgia v. Randolph, 547 U.S. 103 (2006)** addressed whether one resident can consent to a search when another resident objects. ### The Holding **A physically present resident who objects to a search cannot be overridden by another resident's consent.** ### Specific Rule - When a resident is **present and explicitly refuses** consent, police cannot rely on another resident's consent - The objecting resident's Fourth Amendment rights cannot be searched away by a co-occupant - This applies even if the consenting party has equal authority over the premises ### Limitations on Randolph **When Randolph Does NOT Apply:** 1. **Absent Resident** - If the objecting resident is not present, the other can consent - Police need not notify the absent resident - *Fernandez v. Plough*, 27 F.3d 1261 (6th Cir. 1994) - Requires: - Specific evidence likely to be destroyed - Reasonable belief occupants will destroy it - No time to obtain warrant - Example: Flushing drugs down toilet #### 3. **Danger to Persons** - Reasonable belief someone inside needs immediate aid - *United States v. Santana* - Requires: - Valid arrest warrant - Reasonable belief suspect is inside - Exigency of preventing escape #### 5. **Ongoing Criminal Activity** - Preventing continuation of serious criminal activity - Narrowly applied; courts skeptical of this rationale - Requires: - Serious felony (not minor offenses) - Ongoing activity (not completed crime) - Immediate threat ### Exigent Circumstances NOT Recognized **Courts have rejected:** - Mere suspicion of criminal activity - Desire to prevent flight (without specific articulable facts) - General law enforcement needs - Convenience or efficiency - Possibility (without reasonable belief) of evidence destruction ### The Exigency Doctrine and Consent Interaction **Important Principle:** - If exigent circumstances exist, police don't need consent - Conversely, if police claim exigency to justify warrantless entry, they cannot simultaneously rely on consent as justification - *Kentucky v. King*, 563 U.S. 452 (2011): Police cannot create exigency by knocking and announcing ### Scope of Exigent Circumstances Searches - Search limited to areas where the exigency applies - Hot pursuit: Can search areas where suspect might hide - Destruction of evidence: Can search areas where evidence might be destroyed - Aid to persons: Can search areas where person might be --- ## V.
