**Primary location**: Endoplasmic reticulum of hepatocytes (liver cells) - **Other locations**: Intestines, kidneys, lungs, and other tissues - **Name origin**: Called "P450" because they absorb light at 450 nm when bound to carbon monoxide ## CYP3A4: The Major Player ### Significance CYP3A4 is the most abundant and clinically important CYP enzyme, responsible for metabolizing approximately **50-60% of all marketed drugs**. ### Key Characteristics - **Expression**: Highest in liver and small intestine - **Substrate diversity**: Extremely broad substrate specificity - **Inducibility**: Highly inducible by various compounds - **Inhibition**: Susceptible to inhibition by many drugs and compounds ## Mechanism of Xenobiotic Metabolism by CYP3A4 ### The Catalytic Cycle 1. **Substrate Binding** - Xenobiotic enters the enzyme's active site - Displaces water molecule coordinated to heme iron - Iron remains in Fe³⁺ (ferric) state 2. **First Electron Transfer** - NADPH-cytochrome P450 reductase transfers first electron - Fe³⁺ reduced to Fe²⁺ (ferrous state) 3. **Oxygen Binding** - Molecular oxygen (O₂) binds to Fe²⁺ - Forms Fe²⁺-O₂ complex 4. **Second Electron Transfer** - Second electron transferred (from NADPH via reductase or cytochrome b5) - Forms Fe²⁺-O₂²⁻ complex 5. **Protonation and Water Formation** - Two protons added to the complex - One oxygen atom released as H₂O - Forms highly reactive Fe⁵⁺=O intermediate (Compound I) 6. **Substrate Oxidation** - Fe⁵⁺=O transfers oxygen atom to substrate - Various oxidation reactions occur (see below) 7. **Product Release** - Oxidized product released - Enzyme returns to resting Fe³⁺ state ### Types of Oxidation Reactions **Hydroxylation** (most common) - Addition of -OH group to substrate - Example: R-H → R-OH **Epoxidation** - Formation of epoxide groups on double bonds - Example: R-CH=CH-R' → R-CH(O)CH-R' **Dealkylation** - Removal of alkyl groups (methyl, ethyl, etc.) - Example: R-OCH₃ → R-OH + HCHO **Deamination** - Oxidative removal of amino groups - Example: R-NH₂ → R-OH + NH₃ **Sulfoxidation** - Oxidation of sulfur-containing compounds - Example: R-S-R' → R-SO-R' ## Factors Affecting CYP3A4 Activity ### Genetic Polymorphisms - Significant inter-individual variation in expression and activity - Some populations have reduced CYP3A4 function ### Enzyme Induction **Inducers increase CYP3A4 expression:** - Rifampin (classic inducer) - St. John's wort - Carbamazepine - Phenytoin ### Enzyme Inhibition **Inhibitors decrease CYP3A4 activity:** - Ketoconazole (potent inhibitor) - Grapefruit juice - Clarithromycin - Ritonavir ## Clinical Significance ### Drug-Drug Interactions - CYP3A4 inducers can decrease drug efficacy - CYP3A4 inhibitors can increase drug toxicity - Particularly important for drugs with narrow therapeutic windows ### Examples of CYP3A4 Substrates - **Cardiovascular**: Simvastatin, nifedipine - **Immunosuppressants**: Cyclosporine, tacrolimus - **Anticoagulants**: Warfarin (partially) - **Benzodiazepines**: Midazolam, triazolam ### First-Pass Metabolism CYP3A4 in intestinal wall and liver significantly reduces bioavailability of many orally administered drugs through first-pass metabolism. ## Conclusion The CYP3A4 enzyme system represents a sophisticated biological mechanism for xenobiotic metabolism, involving complex electron transfer reactions and diverse oxidation pathways. Understanding this system is crucial for predicting drug interactions, optimizing dosing regimens, and ensuring safe and effective pharmacotherapy.
